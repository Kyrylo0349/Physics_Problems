# Task 10 – Gravitational Time Dilation at the ISS Altitude

## Problem Statement

Determine how much younger an ISS astronaut would be than a twin on Earth purely due to gravitational time dilation after one year.

Assume the ISS orbits at altitude

$$
h = 400 \text{ km}
$$

## Theory

In a weak gravitational field, clocks at higher altitude run slightly faster because the gravitational potential is less negative.

The fractional rate difference between two radii

$$
r_1
$$

and

$$
r_2
$$

is approximately

$$
\frac{\Delta \tau}{t} \approx \frac{GM}{c^2}\left(\frac{1}{r_1} - \frac{1}{r_2}\right)
$$

For the ISS:

$$
r_1 = R_E
$$

$$
r_2 = R_E + h
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

Use

$$
R_E \approx 6.371 \times 10^6 \text{ m}
$$

$$
h = 4.00 \times 10^5 \text{ m}
$$

so

$$
r_2 = 6.771 \times 10^6 \text{ m}
$$

For one year,

$$
t = 365.25 \times 24 \times 3600 \text{ s}
$$

Now compute

$$
\Delta \tau \approx \frac{GM_E}{c^2}\left(\frac{1}{R_E} - \frac{1}{R_E+h}\right)t
$$

This gives

$$
\Delta \tau \approx 0.00130 \text{ s}
$$

## Final Result

Purely due to gravitational time dilation, the astronaut would **not** be younger.

Instead, the astronaut would be older by approximately

$$
0.00130 \text{ s}
$$

or about

$$
1.30 \text{ ms}
$$

after one year.

## Interpretation

Higher altitude means a slightly weaker gravitational field, so clocks there run slightly faster. The ISS astronaut becomes older than the Earth twin from gravity alone, although the kinetic effect works in the opposite direction and is larger.
