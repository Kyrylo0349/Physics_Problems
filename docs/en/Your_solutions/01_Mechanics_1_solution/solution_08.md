# Task 08 – Circular Motion on the Earth's Equator

## Problem Statement

Calculate the centripetal acceleration of a person standing on the Earth's equator. The Earth's radius is approximately

$$
R = 6378 \text{ km}
$$

## Theory

A point moving in uniform circular motion with radius $R$ and angular speed $\omega$ has centripetal acceleration

$$
a_c = \omega^2 R
$$

Since

$$
\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T}
$$

one may also write

$$
a_c = \frac{4\pi^2 R}{T^2}
$$

For the Earth, the period of rotation is approximately

$$
T = 24 \text{ h} = 86400 \text{ s}
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

Convert the radius to meters:

$$
R = 6378 \text{ km} = 6.378 \times 10^6 \text{ m}
$$

Use the formula

$$
a_c = \frac{4\pi^2 R}{T^2}
$$

Substitute the numerical values:

$$
a_c = \frac{4\pi^2 (6.378 \times 10^6)}{(86400)^2}
$$

Evaluating gives

$$
a_c \approx 0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2
$$

## Final Result

The centripetal acceleration at the equator is approximately

$$
a_c \approx 3.37 \times 10^{-2} \text{ m/s}^2
$$

## Interpretation

A person standing at the equator moves in a circle because of the Earth's rotation, so a centripetal acceleration is required toward the Earth's axis. This acceleration is much smaller than gravitational acceleration $g$, which is why its effect is subtle in everyday experience.
